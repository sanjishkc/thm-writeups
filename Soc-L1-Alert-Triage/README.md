  * **L1 Analyst:** First line of defense; triages, filters, and validates incoming alerts.
  * **L2 Analyst:** Conducts deep-dive investigations and handles remediation on escalated issues.
  * **SOC Engineer:** Optimizes detection logic and ensures alerts contain enriched context.
  * **SOC Manager:** Tracks metrics like triage speed and quality to ensure coverage efficiency.

## Task 3: Alert Properties

When an alert triggers, it populates with metadata known as alert properties. This metadata helps SOC analysts reconstruct the timeline, evaluate severity, and identify critical artifacts (IPs, hashes, usernames) without combing blindly through raw logs.

### Important Alert Fields
* **Alert Time:** The timestamp of when the alert rule was triggered (usually a few minutes after the actual log event).
* **Alert Name:** A short description outlining the nature of the rule triggered (e.g., *Unusual VPN Login Location*).
* **Severity:** The priority or urgency level assigned to the alert (**Low**, **Medium**, **High**, **Critical**).
* **Status:** The stage of investigation (**New/Unassigned**, **In Progress**, **Closed/Resolved**).
* **Verdict / Classification:** The final determination of the alert's legitimacy (**True Positive** or **False Positive**).
* **Artifacts/Fields:** Technical evidence and indicators (e.g., source IP, process ID, file paths).

## Task 4: Alert Prioritisation

In a busy Security Operations Center (SOC), a single analyst can be flooded with hundreds of alerts per day. Effective prioritization ensures that critical, active threats are neutralized immediately before analysts spend time digging through low-urgency noise.

### The Standard Triage Hierarchy

A generic, robust workflow for prioritizing the alert queue involves three steps:

1. **Filter Existing Assignments:** Check whether another teammate has already claimed or investigated an alert. Focus exclusively on unassigned, unresolved entries to minimize duplicate effort.
2. **Sort by Severity:** Address **Critical** and **High** alerts first. These are carefully engineered rules that represent massive impact or high-probability compromises compared to Medium or Low warnings.
3. **Sort by Time (Oldest First):** If faced with multiple alerts of equal severity, prioritize the oldest entries. An older breach implies an attacker has already spent time expanding their footprint or exfiltrating data, whereas a brand-new alert could mean the breach is still in its earliest execution phase.

## Task 5: Alert Triage

This task covers the practical, hands-on phase of processing an alert within a SIEM dashboard. The workflow is broken down into three fundamental stages that ensure accountability, thorough analysis, and proper closure.

### The Triage Workflow

1. **Initial Actions:**
   * Take ownership of the assigned alert by assigning it to yourself.
   * Shift the status to **In Progress** to signal to the team that the alert is being actively handled and avoid interference.
   * Familiarise yourself with the alert details like its name, description, and key indicators to build context.

2. **Investigation:**
   * Apply technical knowledge to understand the activity and properly analyse its legitimacy in SIEM or EDR logs.
   * Follow organizational **Workbooks** (playbooks/runbooks) if available. If workbooks are not available, use these key pillars:
     * Understand who is under threat (affected user, hostname, cloud, network, website).
     * Note the action described in the alert (suspicious login, malware, phishing).
     * Review surrounding chronological events looking for suspicious actions shortly before or after the alert.
     * Use threat intelligence platforms or other available resources to verify your thoughts.

3. **Final Actions:**
   * Determine the final verdict: **True Positive (TP)** or **False Positive (FP)**.
   * Prepare a detailed, professional comment explaining your analysis steps and verdict reasoning.
   * Return to the dashboard and move the alert status to **Closed**.
